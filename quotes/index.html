Where You Go, There You Are - John Kabat-Zinn

"By taking a few moments to 'die on purpose' to the rush of time while you are still living, you free yourself to have time for the present. By 'dying' now in this way, you actually become more alive now." (12)

Our desire for change prevents us from fully experiencing the present. Meditation is not about achieving; it’s about being, it’s about submerging yourself so deep into the present that the past is truly the past and the future is truly the future. “Dying on purpose” is just that. Letting go of all your worries, desires, and stress because the truth is the problems you faced when you’re alive will never be fully sorted out when you’re alive: it will only be fully sorted out after you have died. Look around, the problems we face, no matter how much we persist, will always return. Yet, once we die, even without our own efforts, the problems resolve themselves. It is only by “dying on purpose” and letting our “death” per se rid us of such problems that the problems are truly resolved. Throughout the day, ask yourself two questions: “Am I awake?” and “Where is my mind right now?” and when meditating remind yourself every now and then that “This is it”.

"The tack we take in meditation is simply to witness whatever comes up in the mind or the body and to recognize it without condemning it or pursuing it, knowing that our judgements are unavoidable and necessarily limiting thoughts about experience." (56)

"We get caught up in thinking we know what we are seeing and feeling, and in projecting our judgments out onto everything we see off a hairline trigger." (57)

"If you are truly strong, there is little need to emphasize it to yourself or to others. Best to take another tack entirely and direct your attention where you fear most to look. You can do this by allowing yourself to feel, even to cry, to not have to have opinions about everything, to not appear invincible or unfeeling to others, but instead to be in touch with and appropriately open about your feelings. What looks like weakness is actually where your strength lies. And what looks like strength is often weakness, an attempt to cover up fear; this is an act or a facade, however convincing it might appear to others or even to yourself." (66)
