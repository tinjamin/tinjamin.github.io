Integral Test & P-Series

Definition. p-series is a series of the following form:

$$ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^p} $$

Note that the harmonic series is a p-series where $ p=1 $

Improper Integral Theorem

Recall the theorem for the divergence or convergence of an improper integral:

$$ \int_1^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p}dx $$

  • Converges if $ p > 1 $
  • Diverges if $ p \leq 1 $

Example 1

Given

$$ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} = 1 + \frac{1}{2^2} + \frac{1}{3^2} + … $$

We can compare the series to an improper integral of the form

$$ \int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^2}dx $$

Notice however that the bounds of the integral omits the first iteration of the p-series; therefore, when doing the full comparison we must add the first iteration with the improper integral as such:

$$ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} \leq 1 + \int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^2}dx $$

The reason that the p-series is less than or equal to $ 1 + \text{ integral } $ is because the area underneath the curve $ \frac{1}{x^2} $ is continuous whereas the series is discontinuous, thus it makes sense that the integral is larger than the p-series. From here we can evaluate the inequality to get the following:

$$\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} \leq 2$$

Since the partial sum is always increasing (monotonic) and bounded (upper by 2 and lower by 1), we know by theorem that this series must be convergent.

Integral Test

Suppose $ f(x) $ is a positive, decreasing, continuous function on $ [1,\infty) $ with $ f(n)=a_n $. Then the series converges if and only if $ \int_1^\infty f(x)dx$ converges. In essence,

$$ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^p} $$

  • Converges if $ p >1 $
  • Diverges if $ p \leq 1 $

Remarks

The integral and series have the same infinite behavior in terms of convergence or divergence, but they are NOT equal.

$$ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^p} \neq \int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^p}dx $$
